01 Overview

Getting kids to care about
the planet, without lecturing them.

For my university capstone, I designed Splashy, a gamified mobile app that encourages children to adopt eco-friendly habits in their everyday lives. Kids complete real-world quests, earn ocean creatures as rewards, and watch their personal ocean transform from polluted to thriving.

The project ran for one semester and required original user research, defining the product concept, designing and iterating on the UI, and producing a fully interactive Figma prototype. Every character, creature, and illustration was hand-drawn in Adobe Illustrator.

02 The Problem

Kids want to help. They just
don't know how.

Sustainability education for children is largely adult-led and abstract. The gap isn't interest — most kids genuinely care about animals and nature. The gap is agency and immediacy.

01

Abstract impact

Children couldn't see the connection between their actions and environmental outcomes. The cause-and-effect was invisible.

02

No ownership

Existing tools talked at kids rather than with them. Nothing felt personalised or theirs.

03

Delayed rewards

Kids need immediate, tangible feedback. Long-term reward systems don't work for this age group.

04

No real-world link

Apps that lived entirely on-screen missed the point. Behaviour change needed to happen in the physical world.

03 Research

Talking to the actual users.

I conducted semi-structured interviews with six children aged 7 to 11. Rather than asking what features they wanted, I asked about things they already loved: games they played, things they collected, what made them feel proud.

The sessions revealed three consistent patterns that directly shaped the product. The best design decisions weren't mine — they came from listening.

"I want to help but I don't know what to do."

Kids had the desire but no clear pathway. This became the foundation for the quest system, with concrete, achievable tasks tied to real-world actions.

"I love collecting things. Like Pokémon."

The creature collection mechanic came directly from this. Kids are motivated by accumulation and the satisfaction of completing a set.

"I'd want to show my friends my ocean."

The personalised, named ocean taps into pride and identity. Your ocean is uniquely yours and reflects your real-world behaviour over time.

04 User Flow

Real-world action to in-app reward.

The loop between real-world action and reward needed to be short and satisfying, or kids would disengage.

Sign up + questionnaire
View assigned quests
Complete real-world task
Log progress in app
Earn creature + Ecopoints
Ocean updates

Loop must be short. Real-world action → visual reward in 3 taps or fewer.

07 Testing & Iteration

What didn't work, and what I changed.

I tested early prototypes with three of the original research participants. Their responses were direct — kids don't sugarcoat feedback.

Problem found in testing

Quest screen had too much text

Children skipped reading the instructions entirely and just tapped buttons. Original layout assumed a reading level and attention span the age group didn't have.

How I fixed it

Simplified to icon-led cards with one key action

Reduced text by 60%, moved key information into iconography, made the progress bar the dominant visual element. Retested — kids understood the screen within seconds.

Problem found in testing

The ocean didn't feel different enough early on

Children couldn't tell the difference between the dirty and slightly-less-dirty ocean states. The transition felt invisible, removing the reward feeling entirely.

How I fixed it

Added distinct milestone states with creature unlocks

Defined three clear ocean states (polluted, recovering, thriving) with obvious visual differences. Tied creature unlocks to each milestone so progress felt tangible.

Constraints

  • University timeline — one semester from research to prototype, no time for extended testing cycles
  • No parental dashboard in scope, though noted as a critical requirement for any real-world version
  • Quest content manually curated — a scalable generation system would be needed for a real product
  • Long-term engagement mechanics (streaks, social features) deprioritised for MVP scope
08 Outcomes

What the testing showed.

Reward system understood immediately

All three test participants grasped the quest-to-ocean connection without explanation. The visual feedback loop was clear enough to require no instructions.

Collection mechanic drove engagement

Kids asked "what's the next creature?" unprompted — a strong signal the collection mechanic was creating genuine motivation to complete quests.

Simplified UI improved task completion

After the quest screen redesign, children completed the "add progress" action in under 10 seconds, compared to repeated confusion in v1 testing.

09 Reflection

What this taught me.

Designing for children is humbling. Every assumption you have about how users think gets stripped away the moment you actually talk to them.

The biggest lesson was that research isn't optional, it's structural. I started with assumptions about what kids needed. The interviews showed me something completely different. The collection mechanic came from a child mentioning Pokémon. The named ocean came from a kid saying they'd want to show their friends.

I also learned how different it is to design for a non-adult audience. Typography, touch targets, reward timing, information density — everything needs rethinking from the ground up. Simplicity isn't dumbing down, it's doing the hard design work so the user doesn't have to.

If I were to continue this project, the next priorities would be a parental dashboard, scalable quest generation, and longitudinal testing to measure whether the habits actually stuck beyond a first session.
